## SECTION 2 — India Before Colonial Rule [UPSC-M] [TNPSC]

```
INDIA'S ECONOMY IN THE PRE-COLONIAL ERA
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

MUGHAL ERA (1526–1707):
  → India = ~24.4% of world GDP (Angus Maddison estimates, 1700 CE)
     → Largest economy in the world (ahead of China ~22%)
  → Strong agricultural base: land revenue = primary income
  → Vibrant handicraft industries:
     → Cotton textiles: Dhaka (muslin), Surat, Masulipatnam
     → Silk: Varanasi, Murshidabad
     → Metal work: Agra, Lahore
     → Shipbuilding: Surat, Calicut, Masulipatnam
  → Internal trade: well-developed; thriving bazaars
  → External trade: exported textiles, spices, indigo, silk
  → Currency: stable silver rupee (introduced by Sher Shah Suri)
  → Sarraf system: indigenous banking (like early banking)

STATE OF ECONOMY AT ARRIVAL OF EAST INDIA COMPANY (1600):
  → India attracted European traders FOR its manufactured goods
     (NOT as a market to sell TO — this distinction is crucial)
  → European demand for Indian textiles and spices drove trade
  → India had a TRADE SURPLUS — silver flowed IN to India
```

## SECTION 3 — Colonial Economic Exploitation [UPSC-M] [TNPSC] ★★★

### Phases of Colonial Control

```
PHASE 1 — EAST INDIA COMPANY TRADE (1600–1757)
  → EIC traded legitimately; competition with Dutch, Portuguese
  → Battle of Plassey (1757): EIC became political power in Bengal
  → Diwani rights granted (1765): right to collect revenue in Bengal,
    Bihar and Orissa → turning point from trader to ruler

PHASE 2 — EIC TERRITORIAL RULE (1757–1858)
  → Exploitative revenue extraction
  → Great Bengal Famine (1770): 1 crore+ deaths; EIC still collected taxes
  → Forced commercialisation of agriculture (indigo, opium)
  → Destruction of indigenous industries

PHASE 3 — CROWN RULE / BRITISH RAJ (1858–1947)
  → After 1857 Revolt — India under British Crown directly
  → Systematic economic extraction institutionalised
  → Railways, telegraph built primarily for British strategic + economic interests
```

### Drain of Wealth Theory ★★★

| Scholar | Contribution |
|---------|-------------|
| **Dadabhai Naoroji** | Coined **"Drain of Wealth"** theory; Book: *"Poverty and Un-British Rule in India"* (1901); estimated ~£500 million/year drained |
| **R.C. Dutt** | *"Economic History of India"* (1902) — documented deindustrialisation; argued for tariff protection |
| **M.G. Ranade** | Critique of free trade policy; argued for infant industry protection |
| **Romesh Dutt** | Documented how land revenue + trade policy drained wealth |

> **Drain of Wealth = Unrequited exports** — India exported goods but received no equivalent imports in return; money went to England as profits, salaries, pensions of British officials.

### Deindustrialisation ★★★

```
HOW BRITISH POLICIES DESTROYED INDIAN INDUSTRIES:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. FREE TRADE POLICY:
   → British machine-made goods entered India duty-free (or low duty)
   → Indian goods exported to Britain faced HIGH tariffs (up to 80%)
   → Classic example: Manchester cotton cloth vs Dhaka muslin
   → Result: Indian handloom weavers COULD NOT compete → mass unemployment

2. SPECIFIC EXAMPLES:
   → Dhaka muslin (famous globally) → WIPED OUT by Manchester cloth
   → Indian shipbuilding industry → destroyed by British steam ships + policies
   → Indian iron/steel → replaced by British imports
   → Indigo farmers: forced to grow indigo for British; not food crops
     (led to Indigo Revolt 1859-60 in Bengal)

3. SCALE OF DESTRUCTION:
   → Before: India = major manufacturer and exporter
   → After: India became raw material exporter and finished goods importer
   → A complete REVERSAL of the trade pattern

4. WORKFORCE IMPACT:
   → Artisans and weavers pushed back into agriculture
   → Overcrowding in agriculture → subdivision of land
   → Agriculture could not absorb all displaced workers productively
   → Result: Disguised unemployment; fragmented landholdings
```

### Land Revenue Systems ★★★

| System | Region | Feature | Exploitative aspect |
|--------|--------|---------|-------------------|
| **Zamindari** (Permanent Settlement, 1793) | Bengal, Bihar, Orissa | Fixed tax paid by Zamindars to British; Zamindars collected from peasants | Peasants had NO rights; Zamindars became landlords; peasant insecurity |
| **Ryotwari** (1820, Thomas Munro) | Madras, Bombay (most of South India + West India) | Direct settlement between government and individual cultivator (ryot) | High and frequently revised assessments; peasant indebtedness |
| **Mahalwari** (1833, Holt Mackenzie) | NW Provinces, Punjab, Central India | Village community (mahal) collectively responsible for revenue | Village elites exploited common farmers; community debt |

> **TNPSC trap:** Ryotwari = South India (Madras Presidency = TN, AP) ★★★

```
IMPACT OF LAND REVENUE SYSTEMS:
  → High taxes → peasants borrowed from moneylenders (sahukars)
  → Debt trap → land alienation (peasants lost land to moneylenders)
  → Agriculture commercialised for export, not food security
  → Repeated famines: 1876-78 (Great Famine, Madras + Bombay), 1896-97, 1899-1900
  → British famine policy: relief work + free trade (no export ban)
    → Amartya Sen: Famines not due to food shortage but distribution failure
```

### Railways — Dual Character ★★

```
RAILWAYS — BUILT BY BRITISH, FOR BRITISH INTERESTS:

HOW IT HELPED BRITAIN:
  → Connected interior raw material sources to ports (Bombay, Calcutta, Madras)
  → Enabled rapid troop movement (military consolidation)
  → British investors guaranteed 5% return → India paid even if railways lost money
     (Government of India bore the guarantee → burden on Indian taxpayers)
  → Railway equipment imported from Britain → no boost to Indian industry

HOW IT HELPED INDIA (unintended):
  → First connected the subcontinent: unity of market
  → Later used by nationalists (Gandhi's third-class travel)
  → Laid foundation for post-independence industrial expansion

UPSC TRAP: Railways = colonial legacy but also enabled Indian integration
```

## SECTION 4 — Post-Independence Economy: Phase 1 — Planning Era (1947–1991) [ALL] ★★★

### Rationale for Mixed Economy

```
WHY INDIA CHOSE MIXED ECONOMY (not pure capitalism or pure socialism):
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Market failure: Private sector had no capital to build large industries
   (steel, dams, power) → needed public investment

2. Social equity: Pure capitalism would deepen inequality (like colonial era)
   → socialism principles: reduce poverty, achieve equitable growth

3. International context: Cold War — India chose non-alignment
   → Elements of Soviet planning (Five Year Plans) + market democracy

4. Constitutional mandate: Directive Principles (Part IV) direct the state
   to achieve equitable distribution and prevent concentration of wealth

Nehru's vision: "Temples of Modern India" = dams, steel plants, IITs
(Bhakra Nangal dam, Bokaro steel, AIIMS, IITs established 1950s-60s)
```

### Industrial Policy Resolutions ★★

| IPR | Year | Key Feature |
|-----|------|-------------|
| **IPR 1948** | 1948 | First industrial policy; divided industries into 3 categories: state monopoly, mixed, private |
| **IPR 1956** | **1956** ★★★ | **"Economic Constitution of India"** — expanded public sector; industries divided into Schedule A (state), B (mixed), C (private); reaffirmed socialist pattern |
| **New Industrial Policy** | **1991** | Abolished industrial licensing (except 18 → 5 → 3 industries); dismantled MRTP; opened to FDI |

### Five Year Plans — At a Glance ★★★

```
FIVE YEAR PLANS SUMMARY:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1st Plan (1951-56) — AGRICULTURE focus
  Architect: P.C. Mahalanobis (drafted framework)
  Priority: Agriculture + irrigation (post-Partition food crisis)
  Achievement: 3.6% growth (target 2.1%) ✓ Exceeded

2nd Plan (1956-61) — HEAVY INDUSTRY focus (Mahalanobis Model)
  Model: Feldman-Mahalanobis model (invest in capital goods)
  Priority: Steel plants (Bhilai, Rourkela, Durgapur)
  "Socialist Pattern of Society" declared
  Growth: 4.3% ≈ target

3rd Plan (1961-66) — SELF-RELIANCE target
  Disrupted: China War (1962), Pakistan War (1965), drought (1965-66)
  Growth: 2.8% — failed badly
  Followed by: "Plan Holiday" 1966-69 (Annual Plans only)

4th Plan (1969-74)
  Focus: Growth with stability and social justice (Indira Gandhi)
  14 banks nationalised (1969) ★★★ — major economic event
  Green Revolution impact started showing

5th Plan (1974-79) — POVERTY & EMPLOYMENT focus
  Slogan: "Garibi Hatao" (from 4th plan, continued)
  Introduced 20-Point Programme
  Minimum Needs Programme launched

6th Plan (1980-85) — POVERTY ALLEVIATION
  Focus: Direct poverty alleviation + infrastructure
  IRDP (Integrated Rural Development Programme)
  TRYSEM, NREP schemes
  Growth: 5.7% — first major success

7th Plan (1985-90) — FOOD, WORK, PRODUCTIVITY
  Rajiv Gandhi era; modernisation emphasis
  Computer policy liberalised
  Growth: 6.0% — best so far at that point

Annual Plans (1990-92) — due to political instability

8th Plan (1992-97) — LPG REFORMS era
  Post-1991 crisis; Narasimha Rao + Manmohan Singh
  Human Development approach (influenced by Amartya Sen / UNDP)
  Fastest GDP growth: 6.8% achieved

9th Plan (1997-02) — GROWTH WITH SOCIAL JUSTICE
  Hindu rate of growth syndrome crept back
  Growth: 5.4% — missed 6.5% target

10th Plan (2002-07) — 8% GROWTH target
  India Shining phase; IT boom
  Growth: 7.8% ≈ target

11th Plan (2007-12) — FASTER AND INCLUSIVE GROWTH
  First time "Inclusive" added
  Social sector emphasis: health + education expenditure up
  Growth: 7.9% ≈ target

12th Plan (2012-17) — FASTER, MORE INCLUSIVE, SUSTAINABLE
  "Sustainable" added for first time
  Growth: 6.8% — missed 8.2% target (GDP slowdown, NPA crisis)

→ Planning Commission ABOLISHED January 2015
→ NITI Aayog replaced it (National Institution for Transforming India)
→ No more Five Year Plans; now 15-Year Vision + 7-Year Strategy
```

### Green Revolution ★★★

```
GREEN REVOLUTION (1960s–1970s):
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

TRIGGER: 1965-66 severe drought + war → India dependent on US food aid
  (PL-480 wheat imports — called "ship-to-mouth" existence)

KEY ELEMENTS:
  → HYV (High Yielding Variety) seeds
  → Chemical fertilisers (urea) + pesticides
  → Irrigation expansion
  → Institutional credit to farmers

CROPS: Wheat first, then Rice
STATES: Punjab, Haryana, Western UP — initially
        (Tamil Nadu, AP — for rice later)

KEY PEOPLE:
  → Norman Borlaug (Mexican agronomist): Nobel 1970; developed HYV wheat
  → M.S. Swaminathan: "Father of Green Revolution in India" ★★★
  → C. Subramaniam: Agriculture Minister who implemented it

IMPACT:
  ✓ India achieved food self-sufficiency by early 1970s
  ✓ Punjab/Haryana became "granaries of India"
  ✓ Wheat production: 11 MT (1960) → 20 MT (1970) → 55 MT (1990)
  ✓ PDS (Public Distribution System) established — food security architecture

NEGATIVE IMPACTS (2nd Generation Problems):
  ✗ Ecological: soil degradation, groundwater depletion (Punjab crisis)
  ✗ Inequality: only large farmers benefited; small farmers left out
  ✗ Regional imbalance: Punjab/Haryana vs Bihar/Orissa
  ✗ Crop imbalance: wheat + rice promoted; pulses, millets neglected
  ✗ Punjab "agrarian crisis": water table fallen 1 metre/year ★

2nd Green Revolution: current push for millets, pulses, eastern India
  → International Year of Millets 2023 (India proposed) ★★
```

### White Revolution & Operation Flood ★★

```
WHITE REVOLUTION (Operation Flood, 1970–1996):
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Father: Verghese Kurien — "Milkman of India" ★★★
Organisation: National Dairy Development Board (NDDB), 1965
Implemented: AMUL model (Anand Milk Union Limited, Gujarat)

AMUL MODEL:
  → Village cooperative → District union → State federation
  → Dairy farmers own the cooperative
  → Pooled resources, shared infrastructure, strong brand
  → Anand, Gujarat — headquartered (gives "Anand Pattern" name)

IMPACT:
  → India: world's LARGEST milk producer ★★★
  → 1950: 17 MT → 2024: ~230+ MT milk production
  → Doubled farmers' incomes in participating cooperatives
  → Model replicated: NDDB expanded to oilseeds (Yellow Revolution)

Yellow Revolution: oilseeds (1986)
Blue Revolution: fisheries (fish production)
```

### Bank Nationalisation 1969 ★★★

```
14 MAJOR BANKS NATIONALISED in July 1969 (Indira Gandhi):
  → Purpose: channelise credit to priority sectors (agriculture, small industry)
  → Banks had been serving only urban elite and large businesses
  → After nationalisation: branch expansion in rural areas
  → Enabled Green Revolution credit flow + poverty alleviation schemes

6 MORE banks nationalised in 1980 → total 20 nationalised banks
Regional Rural Banks (RRBs) established 1975 for rural credit ★
```

## SECTION 5 — Post-Independence Economy: Phase 2 — LPG Reforms (1991–2014) [ALL] ★★★

### The 1991 Balance of Payments Crisis

```
1991 CRISIS — TIMELINE & CAUSES:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

CAUSES:
  → Gulf War (1990-91): oil prices doubled; India = 85% oil import dependent
  → Gulf remittances dried up (NRI funds withdrawn in panic)
  → Fiscal profligacy: government borrowing to fund subsidies
  → Trade deficit worsened
  → Political instability (multiple governments 1989-91)

THE CRISIS MOMENT:
  → Forex reserves fell to ~$1.2 billion (barely 2 weeks of imports)
  → India MORTGAGED 67 tonnes of gold to Bank of England + UBS
    (physically airlifted to London — national humiliation)
  → IMF bailout sought with conditions attached

REFORM ARCHITECTS:
  PM: P.V. Narasimha Rao ★★★ (Congress)
  FM: Dr. Manmohan Singh ★★★ (presented Budget July 24, 1991)
  Chief Economic Advisor: Montek Singh Ahluwalia
  RBI Governor: R.N. Malhotra

BUDGET SPEECH (1991): "No power on earth can stop an idea whose time has come"
  → Quote from Victor Hugo — used by Manmohan Singh ★
```

### LPG Reforms — Details ★★★

```
L — LIBERALISATION:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  → Industrial Licensing abolished for most industries
    (from 84 licensed → 18 → 5 → now 3: defence, alcohol, atomic energy + some)
  → MRTP Act (Monopolies and Restrictive Trade Practices, 1969) diluted
    (replaced later by Competition Act 2002 + CCI)
  → Import licensing abolished; import duties reduced
  → Rupee made convertible on Current Account (1994)
  → Capital markets liberalised: SEBI strengthened (SEBI Act 1992)

P — PRIVATISATION:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  → Disinvestment of PSUs began
  → Disinvestment Commission set up (1996)
  → Air India (finally privatised 2022 — Tata Group) ★★
  → BPCL, HPCL, LIC IPO — partial disinvestment
  → Strategic sale vs minority sale debate
  → NPA recognition + resolution (IBC 2016 later)

G — GLOBALISATION:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  → India joined WTO (World Trade Organisation) — 1995 ★★★
    (GATT successor; India was GATT member from 1948)
  → FDI allowed in most sectors:
    → Automatic route: up to certain % without government approval
    → Government route: approval needed for sensitive sectors
  → Exchange rate market-determined (earlier fixed/managed)
  → Import duty reductions: textile, consumer goods

IMPACT OF LPG REFORMS:
  → GDP growth: 5-6% (1990s) → 7-9% (2003-2012)
  → IT/ITES boom: India became global software hub
    → Infosys, Wipro, TCS → global companies
    → Bengaluru = Silicon Valley of India
  → Middle class expansion: 250 million middle class by 2010s
  → FDI: negligible (1991) → $70+ billion (2020s)
  → India moved from 12th largest → 5th largest economy
  → Poverty: 45% (1993-94) → 22% (2011-12) → <15% (2023)
```

## SECTION 6 — Post-Independence Economy: Phase 3 — Structural Reforms (2014–present) [ALL] ★★★

```
KEY REFORMS 2014-PRESENT:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. GST — GOODS AND SERVICES TAX (July 1, 2017) ★★★
   → "One Nation One Tax" — replaced 17 indirect taxes + 23 cesses
   → Constitutional Amendment: 101st Amendment Act, 2016
   → GST Council: Finance Minister chairs; all state FMs members
   → Structure: CGST (Centre) + SGST (State) + IGST (inter-state)
   → 4 rates: 5%, 12%, 18%, 28% + cess on demerit goods
   → Largest tax reform since Independence
   → Impact: formalisation of economy; unified market; 1.4+ crore GST registrations

2. IBC — INSOLVENCY AND BANKRUPTCY CODE (2016) ★★
   → Single law for resolving insolvency (replaced 12 fragmented laws)
   → Time-bound resolution: 180 + 90 days
   → NCLT (National Company Law Tribunal) handles corporate insolvency
   → DRT (Debt Recovery Tribunal) for individual/partnership
   → IBBI: Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India (regulator)
   → Impact: NPAs resolved; ₹3+ lakh crore recovered; credit discipline

3. JAM TRINITY ★★★
   J = Jan Dhan Yojana (Aug 2014): 53+ crore zero-balance bank accounts
   A = Aadhaar: 12-digit unique biometric ID; 130+ crore enrolled
   M = Mobile: ubiquitous smartphones + UPI (Unified Payments Interface)
   → DBT (Direct Benefit Transfer): subsidies directly to beneficiary
   → Eliminated middlemen; saved ₹2.23 lakh crore (govt estimate, cumulative)
   → UPI transactions: 100+ crore/month; global model for digital payments

4. MAKE IN INDIA (Sept 2014) + PLI SCHEMES
   → Make in India: target manufacturing = 25% of GDP (from 17-18%)
   → PLI (Production Linked Incentive):
     → 14 sectors; total outlay: ~₹2 lakh crore over 5 years
     → Sectors: Mobile phones, API/pharma, textiles, automobiles/EVs,
                food processing, white goods, batteries, solar modules,
                specialty steel, telecom products, advanced chemistry cells
     → Impact: Apple (Foxconn) in India; Samsung India expansion;
               India = 2nd largest mobile phone manufacturer ★

5. DEMONETISATION (Nov 8, 2016)
   → ₹500 + ₹1000 notes demonetised (86% of currency by value)
   → Goals: black money, counterfeit notes, digital payments push
   → Short-term GDP impact: 2016-17 growth fell to 8.3% → 7.2% (debate)

6. ATMANIRBHAR BHARAT (2020)
   → Covid relief + self-reliance manufacturing push
   → ₹20 lakh crore package (10% of GDP — largely liquidity)
   → Defence indigenisation: positive indigenisation list
       (100+ items that CANNOT be imported; must be made in India)
   → Space sector opened to private players (IN-SPACe)

7. NATIONAL MONETISATION PIPELINE (NMP, 2021)
   → Monetise ₹6 lakh crore of government infrastructure assets (4 years)
   → Roads (NHAI), railways, airports, power transmission
   → NOT privatisation; ownership stays with govt; operations leased

8. NATIONAL INFRASTRUCTURE PIPELINE (NIP)
   → ₹111 lakh crore investment in infrastructure by 2025
   → Extended; now ₹143 lakh crore by 2030
   → Sectors: Roads, Railways, Urban, Ports, Airports, Digital, Energy
```

## SECTION 7 — Indian Economy: Sectors & Structure [UPSC-P] [TNPSC] ★★★

### Sector Overview

```
THREE SECTORS OF INDIAN ECONOMY:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

PRIMARY SECTOR (Agriculture + Mining + Forestry + Fishing):
  GDP share  : ~17% | Employment: ~45-47%
  KEY PROBLEM: Employment share >> GDP share → disguised unemployment
  Sub-sectors:
    Agriculture: 14-15% GDP
    Mining & quarrying: ~2-3% GDP
    Forestry + fishing: ~1% each

SECONDARY SECTOR (Manufacturing + Construction + Electricity):
  GDP share: ~27-28% | Employment: ~25%
  Manufacturing: ~17-18% GDP (target 25% under Make in India)
  Construction: ~8-9% GDP
  Challenge: Premature deindustrialisation — services boom before mfg. peak

TERTIARY SECTOR (Services):
  GDP share: ~55-57% | Employment: ~25-30%
  IT/ITES: $250+ billion sector; India = global back-office
  Financial services, trade, transport, healthcare, education
  KEY ISSUE: Large informal component in services

STRUCTURAL TRANSFORMATION ISSUE:
  Normal path: Agriculture → Manufacturing → Services
  India's path: Agriculture → Services (SKIP manufacturing)
  → Called "premature deindustrialisation"
  → China had manufacturing surge (made it world's factory)
  → India missed manufacturing absorption of surplus farm labour
  → PLI schemes: corrective attempt
```

## SECTION 8 — Indian Agriculture [UPSC-P] [TNPSC]

```
INDIAN AGRICULTURE — KEY FACTS:
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LAND USE:
  → Net Sown Area: ~140 million hectares (largest in world after USA)

  → 1st: MILK, PULSES, SPICES, JUTE, BANANA, MANGO, PAPAYA, GINGER ★★★

  Kharif  : Sown: June-July | Harvested: Sept-Oct
            Crops: Rice, Cotton, Maize, Jowar, Bajra, Tur dal, Soybean
  Rabi    : Sown: Oct-Nov | Harvested: Feb-Mar
            Crops: Wheat, Barley, Mustard, Gram (Chana), Linseed

  → Canal irrigation: largest source (Uttar Pradesh, Punjab)

  → e-NAM: electronic National Agriculture Market; 1,361 mandis linked ★
  → FPO (Farmer Producer Organisations): 10,000 FPOs to be formed by 2027-28
  → PM Annadata Aay SanraksHan Abhiyan (PM-AASHA): MSP procurement

FARM INCOME CHALLENGE:
  → Agriculture = 17% GDP but 45-47% employment
  → Average farm size: 1.08 hectares (declining; sub-economic holdings)
  → 80%+ farmers = small/marginal (< 2 ha)
  → Solution needed: value chains, non-farm rural employment, agri-exports
```
