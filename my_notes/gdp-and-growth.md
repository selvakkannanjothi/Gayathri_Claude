## SECTION 2 — GDP Formula & Components [ALL]

### Expenditure Method (Most Important for UPSC/TNPSC)

```
GDP = C + I + G + (X – M)

  C  =  Private Final Consumption Expenditure (household spending)
  I  =  Gross Fixed Capital Formation (investment by businesses)
  G  =  Government Final Consumption Expenditure
  X  =  Exports
  M  =  Imports
  X–M =  Net Exports (can be + or –)

INDIA'S COMPONENT SHARE (approx.):
  C ≈ 57%  |  I ≈ 32%  |  G ≈ 11%  |  Net Exports ≈ −1 to −2%
  → India is primarily a CONSUMPTION-driven economy
```

## SECTION 3 — National Income Chain Flowchart [ALL] ★★★

```
NATIONAL INCOME CHAIN
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

        GDP at MARKET PRICE
              │
              │ – Indirect Taxes + Subsidies
              ▼
        GDP at FACTOR COST
              │
              │ – Depreciation (Capital Consumption Allowance)
              ▼
        NDP at Factor Cost
        (Net Domestic Product)
              │
              │ + Net Factor Income from Abroad (NFIA)
              ▼
        NNP at Factor Cost
        = NATIONAL INCOME
              │
              │ ÷ Population
              ▼
        PER CAPITA INCOME
        (Per Capita NNP)

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
HORIZONTAL CHAIN (GDP → GNP):

  GDP  + Net Factor Income from Abroad (NFIA)  =  GNP
  GNP  – Depreciation                          =  NNP = National Income
  NNP  ÷ Population                            =  Per Capita Income
  GDP  – Depreciation                          =  NDP

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

MEMORY TRICK:
  "GDP – D = NDP ; GDP + N = GNP ; GNP – D = NNP ; NNP ÷ P = PCI"
   D = Depreciation | N = Net Factor Income Abroad | P = Population
```

## SECTION 4 — Key Concepts & Differences [UPSC-P] [TNPSC] ★★★

### GDP vs GNP vs GNI vs NNP

| Term | Formula | What it measures |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| **GDP** | C+I+G+(X-M) | All production **within** the country (residents + foreigners inside) |
| **GNP** | GDP + NFIA | Production by **Indian citizens** anywhere in the world |
| **GNI** | Same as GNP | GNI = GNP (modern term used by World Bank) |
| **NNP** | GNP – Depreciation | National income after accounting for wear & tear |
| **NDP** | GDP – Depreciation | Domestic production after depreciation |
| **Per Capita Income** | NNP ÷ Population | Average income per person |

> **Exam trap:** HDI uses **GNI per capita** (not GDP). World Bank ranks countries by **GNI per capita** (not GDP). TNPSC loves this trap.

### Nominal vs Real GDP ★★★

| | Nominal GDP | Real GDP |
|---|---|---|
| **Prices used** | Current year prices | Base year prices (2011-12) |
| **Reflects** | Both output + price changes | Only output (volume) changes |
| **Used for** | Comparing current size | Comparing growth over time |
| **Problem** | Inflation inflates the number | True economic performance |

```
GDP DEFLATOR = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100

  If GDP Deflator rises → inflation in the economy
  Real GDP = (Nominal GDP / GDP Deflator) × 100

NOTE: GDP Deflator ≠ CPI
  → GDP Deflator covers ALL goods & services
  → CPI covers only a basket of consumer goods
```

### GDP at Market Price vs Factor Cost

```
GDP at MARKET PRICE  =  GDP at FACTOR COST  +  Indirect Taxes  –  Subsidies

Market Price: what the CONSUMER pays (includes taxes)
Factor Cost:  what the PRODUCER receives (excludes taxes, includes subsidies)

UPSC trap: Government budget, spending data are at MARKET PRICES
           National income accounts: now India reports at MARKET PRICES
           (India changed from Factor Cost to Market Price in 2015 revision)
```

## SECTION 5 — Three Methods of Measuring GDP [UPSC-P]

```
METHOD 1 — EXPENDITURE METHOD
  GDP = C + I + G + (X–M)
  Add up all spending in the economy
  ★ Most commonly used; easiest to understand

METHOD 2 — INCOME METHOD (Factor Income Method)
  GDP = Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit + Mixed Income
  Add up all factor incomes earned in production
  ★ Shows distribution of national income

METHOD 3 — PRODUCTION METHOD (Value Added Method)
  GDP = Sum of Value Added at each stage of production
  Value Added = Output value – Input value (avoids double counting)
  Example:
    Farmer sells wheat ₹10 → Value Added = ₹10
    Miller sells flour ₹18 → Value Added = ₹8 (18–10)
    Baker sells bread ₹25 → Value Added = ₹7 (25–18)
    GDP contribution = ₹10 + ₹8 + ₹7 = ₹25 ✓ (= final product price)

ALL THREE METHODS GIVE THE SAME ANSWER (in theory)
→ Called "three-way identity" of national income
```

## SECTION 6 — India's GDP Growth History [UPSC-M] [TNPSC]

### Pre-Independence Baseline

| Period | India's share of World GDP | Condition |
|--------|--------------------------|-----------|
| 1700 CE | ~**24%** (Maddison data) | Largest economy |
| 1900 CE | ~**8%** | Colonial drain — Dadabhai Naoroji |
| 1947 | ~**4%** | At Independence |
| 2023 | ~**3.5%** (nominal) / **7%+** (PPP) | Recovering |

### Post-Independence Growth Phases

```
PHASE 1 — MIXED ECONOMY / PLANNING ERA (1951–1991)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  Growth rate: ~3.5% p.a. average (nicknamed "Hindu rate of growth")
  Coined by: Economist Raj Krishna (1978)

  Strategy:
    → Import Substitution Industrialisation (ISI)
    → Public sector-led investment (steel, power, dams)
    → Five Year Plans (Planning Commission, 1950)
    → Green Revolution (1960s-70s) — boosted agriculture GDP
    → Licence Raj — private sector controlled by permits

  Key GDP milestones:
    → 1st Five Year Plan (1951-56): 3.6% growth
    → 2nd Plan (1956-61): 4.3% (Nehru-Mahalanobis model)
    → 1966-67: Worst drought year → negative growth
    → 1979-80: −5.2% growth (drought + oil shock)

PHASE 2 — LPG REFORMS ERA (1991–2014)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  Trigger: BoP Crisis 1991 — forex reserves = 2 weeks of imports
  India pledged gold to IMF → conditionality → LPG reforms
  Architects: PM P.V. Narasimha Rao + FM Dr. Manmohan Singh

  Reform pillars:
    L = Liberalisation → dismantled Licence Raj, MRTP Act repealed
    P = Privatisation → PSU disinvestment began
    G = Globalisation → FDI allowed, trade barriers reduced

  Growth impact:
    → Average GDP growth: 6–7% (1990s); 7–9% (2003-2012)
    → IT/ITES boom: services overtook manufacturing
    → India = 12th largest → 10th → 9th economy

  High growth decade (2003-2012 = "India Shining"):
    2003-04: 8.1% | 2004-05: 7.0% | 2005-06: 9.5%
    2006-07: 9.6% | 2007-08: 9.3% | 2008-09: 6.7% (Global crisis)
    2010-11: 8.9% | 2011-12: 6.7%

PHASE 3 — STRUCTURAL REFORMS (2014 onwards)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  Key reforms:
    → GST (July 2017): "One Nation One Tax" — unified market
    → IBC (2016): Insolvency & Bankruptcy Code — NPA resolution
    → JAM Trinity: Jan Dhan + Aadhaar + Mobile — financial inclusion
    → Make in India, PLI schemes, infrastructure push

  Growth trend:
    2014-15: 7.4% | 2015-16: 8.0% | 2016-17: 8.3%
    2017-18: 7.0% (GST disruption) | 2018-19: 6.1%
    2019-20: 4.0% (slowdown) | 2020-21: −6.6% (COVID)
    2021-22: 9.1% (base effect) | 2022-23: 7.2%
    2023-24: 8.2% ★★★ (fastest growing major economy)
    2024-25: 6.4% (NSO 1st Advance Estimate, Jan 2025)

  FY 2024-25 QUARTERLY BREAKDOWN ★ [UPSC-P]:
    Q1 (Apr–Jun 2024): 6.7%
    Q2 (Jul–Sep 2024): 5.4% ← Notable slowdown (urban consumption + manufacturing weakness)
    Q3 (Oct–Dec 2024): 6.4% ← Recovery
    Q4 (Jan–Mar 2025): ~7.0% (implied; NSO provisional)
    Full Year FY25  :  6.4% (NSO Advance Estimate)

  WHY Q2 SLOWDOWN MATTERS (UPSC Mains angle):
    → Private consumption decelerated in urban areas
    → Government capex was front-loaded in FY24; moderated in Q2 FY25
    → Manufacturing GVA growth slowed to ~2.2% that quarter
    → Recovery in Q3 driven by festive season + government spending pickup
```

## SECTION 8 — GDP Growth Drivers [UPSC-M] [TNPSC]

```
SUPPLY-SIDE DRIVERS (production factors)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. CAPITAL FORMATION (Investment)
   → Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF) ~32% of GDP
   → Higher investment → more factories, infrastructure → higher output
   → India's challenge: private investment slow since 2012
   → Solution: Government capital expenditure (₹11.21 lakh crore in Budget 2025-26 ★)

2. LABOUR FORCE
   → India: 1.4 billion people → demographic dividend
   → Working age population (15–64): ~68% = world's largest
   → But: skill mismatch, low female LFPR (37%)
   → Dividend window: 2020–2040 (after this: ageing begins)

3. TECHNOLOGY & PRODUCTIVITY (TFP — Total Factor Productivity)
   → IT/digital revolution → services boom
   → PLI (Production Linked Incentive) schemes → manufacturing push
   → Sectors: semiconductors, EVs, solar, defence

4. NATURAL RESOURCES
   → India: coal (3rd largest reserves), iron ore, manganese
   → But over-dependence on oil imports → $150+ billion/year
   → Energy transition → renewables (500 GW target by 2030)

DEMAND-SIDE DRIVERS (spending)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. CONSUMPTION (C) ≈ 57% of GDP
   → Driven by: rising middle class, urbanisation, credit growth
   → Rural: driven by agri income, MGNREGS, PM Kisan
   → Urban: IT/service sector income, aspirational spending

2. INVESTMENT (I) ≈ 32% of GDP
   → Public capex replacing private investment (crowding in effect)
   → FDI inflow: $70–80 billion/year
   → Capex multiplier: ₹1 government capex → ₹2.5+ GDP boost

3. GOVERNMENT SPENDING (G) ≈ 11% of GDP
   → Infrastructure: roads, railways, ports, airports
   → Social spending: MGNREGS, PM Awas, Jal Jeevan Mission

4. NET EXPORTS (X–M) ≈ –1 to –2% GDP
   → India: chronic trade deficit (imports > exports)
   → India exports: IT services, gems/jewellery, pharma, textiles
   → India imports: crude oil (largest), electronics, machinery
```

## SECTION 9 — GDP Growth Challenges [UPSC-M]

```
CHALLENGE 1 — PRIVATE INVESTMENT SLOWDOWN
  → Corporate investment weak since 2012 (NPA crisis, demand slowdown)
  → Capacity utilisation: rose to ~75% in 2023-24 → investment recovery expected
  → IBC + bank recapitalisation → NPAs declined → credit flow improving

CHALLENGE 2 — AGRICULTURE-EMPLOYMENT GAP
  → 45-50% employed in agriculture → 17% GDP contribution
  → Low farm productivity → low rural incomes → low consumption
  → Solution: non-farm rural employment (MGNREGS), crop diversification,
    value chains, farmer producer organisations (FPOs)

CHALLENGE 3 — SERVICES-LED GROWTH (Premature Deindustrialisation)
  → India skipped the manufacturing surge that China/South Korea had
  → Manufacturing = 18% GDP (target: 25% by 2025 under Make in India)
  → Without manufacturing jobs, services cannot absorb all workers
  → PLI schemes: ₹2 lakh crore incentives to boost manufacturing

CHALLENGE 4 — INFORMAL ECONOMY
  → 80-90% of workforce in informal sector (no social security)
  → Informal economy = ~50% of GDP (unrecorded in NAS partly)
  → COVID disproportionately hurt informal workers
  → GST formalisation: 1.4 crore+ new GST registrations

CHALLENGE 5 — EXTERNAL VULNERABILITY
  → Oil import dependence: ~85% of oil needs imported
  → CAD (Current Account Deficit): ~1.5–2% GDP
  → Rupee depreciation risk when dollar strengthens

CHALLENGE 6 — INEQUALITY
  → GDP growth ≠ equitable growth
  → Top 1% earns 22% of national income (World Inequality Report 2022)
  → India's Gini coefficient ~0.35 (moderate but worsening)
  → GDP per capita (~$2,400) masks deep rural-urban divide
```

## SECTION 10 — GDP vs Human Welfare Debate [UPSC-M]

```
WHY GDP IS NOT A COMPLETE MEASURE OF WELFARE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LIMITATIONS OF GDP:
  1. Ignores inequality — high GDP with high Gini = many poor
  2. Ignores unpaid work — household work, care work (done mainly by women)
  3. Ignores environmental damage — pollution-producing GDP is not "real" wealth
  4. Ignores leisure — overwork to get high GDP is not welfare
  5. Ignores distribution — per capita GDP is average, not median

EXAMPLES:
  → Kerala: lower per capita GDP than Goa/Delhi but HIGHEST HDI
  → Bihar: low GDP but social indicators slightly improving
  → Saudi Arabia: high GDP per capita but low gender equality

ALTERNATIVES TO GDP:
  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Measure          │ What it adds over GDP                    │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ HDI              │ Health + Education + Income (UNDP)       │
  │ IHDI             │ HDI adjusted for inequality              │
  │ GPI (Genuine     │ Subtracts inequality + pollution +       │
  │  Progress Ind.)  │ crime costs from GDP                     │
  │ Happiness Index  │ World Happiness Report — subjective      │
  │ GNH (Bhutan)     │ Gross National Happiness                 │
  │ Green GDP        │ GDP minus environmental depletion cost   │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────┘

INDIA'S APPROACH:
  → "Sabka Saath, Sabka Vikas, Sabka Vishwas" — growth with inclusion
  → Aspirational Districts Programme: 112 low-HDI districts targeted
  → Viksit Bharat @2047: "Developed India" = not just GDP but quality of life
```

## SECTION 15 — Memory Tricks [ALL]

### GDP Formula
> **"CIGX–M"** = **C**onsumption + **I**nvestment + **G**overnment + e**X**ports – i**M**ports

### National Income Chain
> **"GDP − D = NDP ; GDP + N = GNP ; GNP − D = NNP ; NNP ÷ P = PCI"**
> D = Depreciation | N = Net Factor Income from Abroad | P = Population

### Nominal vs Real
> **"NOMINAL = NOW (current prices); REAL = REAL performance (base year prices)"**
> GDP Deflator = thermometer of inflation in GDP

### India GDP Rank
> **"USA China Germany Japan INDIA"** — 1, 2, 3, 4, **5**
> At PPP: **USA China INDIA** — 1, 2, **3**

### Three Methods
> **"EIP"** — **E**xpenditure, **I**ncome, **P**roduction (all give same answer)

### Growth Rates to Remember
> **"8.2 in 23-24; 6.4 in 24-25"** — FY24 peak, FY25 moderation
> At PPP: India 3rd | Nominal: India 5th → will be 4th by ~2026-27

### Hindu Rate of Growth
> **"3.5 is Hindu; 8 is New India"** — Pre-reform vs post-reform target
